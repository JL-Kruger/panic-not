Common Attack Patterns Online

Pattern recognition is your best defense

üé£

The Grooming Gradual

What it looks like: Starts friendly ("I get you"), slowly introduces personal questions over weeks, moves to private messaging, shares "secrets", eventually asks for photos or meetings.

Red flags:
  • Adult taking unusual interest in child's problems
  • Requests to "keep our friendship secret"
  • Age-inappropriate gifts or attention
  • Pressure to move off-platform
Defense: Trust your gut. Real friends don't ask you to keep friendships secret from family.
üí∞

The Scam Spiral

What it looks like: Too-good-to-be-true offers (free Robux!), fake giveaways, "click to claim", password requests to "verify", chain messages.

Red flags:
  • Urgency pressure ("Expires in 1 hour!")
  • Requests for passwords or payment info
  • Links to sites that don't match official domains
  • Grammar/spelling mistakes in "official" messages
Defense: If it's free, you're the product. Never share passwords.
üé≠

The Fake Friend

What it looks like: Account pretending to be your friend ("My old account got hacked"), asks for help, requests verification codes, wants personal info.

Red flags:
  • Can't video chat to "prove" identity
  • Unusual phrasing or mistakes real friend wouldn't make
  • Urgency and pressure tactics
  • Wants to bypass normal communication
Defense: Verify through a different channel. If "Jake" messages on Discord, call Jake's phone.
üî•

The Drama Amplifier

What it looks like: Screenshots of "what someone said about you", polls to pick sides, pressure to participate in harassment, artificial urgency around social conflicts.

Red flags:
  • Content designed to make you angry or hurt
  • Pressure to share or respond immediately
  • Information that seems designed to damage relationships
  • Asks you to take sides in conflicts you weren't part of
Defense: Pause before engaging. Screenshots can be edited. Drama thrives on audience‚Äîdon't feed it.
üì∏

The Content Weaponizer

What it looks like: Taking innocent photos and creating fake explicit versions (AI "nudify"), threatening to share embarrassing content unless you comply, creating fake evidence, impersonating you.

Red flags:
  • Threats: "Send me X or I'll share these photos"
  • Content of you that you never created
  • Voice messages you never sent
  • Pressure based on "evidence" that doesn't match memory
Defense: Document and report immediately. Never comply with demands. Most countries treat this as criminal.
üß™

The Curiosity Trap

What it looks like: "You won't believe what this quiz reveals!", "Click to see who viewed your profile!", personality tests asking invasive questions.

Red flags:
  • Asks for more permissions than needed
  • Requires sharing on timeline before results
  • Too many personal questions for a silly quiz
  • Third-party app wants access to entire account
Defense: Legitimate services don't need access to your entire life. Assume data will be sold.

Universal Defense Strategies

For Teens:

01
The Pause Button: When something feels urgent or uncomfortable, that's your signal to STOP and tell an adult
02
The Screenshot Strategy: Document before blocking‚Äîevidence helps protect others
03
The Verification Game: Real friends won't mind if you verify them through another channel
04
The Privacy Pyramid: Share less = fewer attack surfaces. Personal info is currency‚Äîspend wisely
05
The Gut Check: Your instincts exist for a reason. Uncomfortable = worth investigating

For Parents:

01
Regular Check-ins: Make "what's happening online" as normal as "how was school"
02
No-judgment Reporting: Kids won't tell you about problems if they fear punishment
03
Platform Familiarity: Actually know what Discord, Roblox, TikTok are and how they work
04
Trust Patterns: Notice changes in secrecy, mood, or device-guarding behavior
05
Resource Ready: Know where to report (platform, school, police) before you need to

When to Escalate

üì¢ Tell a Trusted Adult Immediately

  • Anyone asks to keep conversations secret
  • Someone requests photos, personal info, or meetings
  • You receive threats or sexual content
  • Someone impersonates you or creates fake content
  • You feel scared, pressured, or confused

üö© Report to Platforms

  • Violations of community guidelines
  • Harassment of you or others
  • Inappropriate content in age-restricted spaces
  • Scams or fraud attempts

üö® Contact Authorities

  • Adults request meetings or sexual content from minors
  • Threats of violence occur
  • Blackmail or extortion happens
  • Deepfake content of minors appears
  • Criminal activity is solicited

Graduated Independence Model

Protection through competence, not control

11-13

Training Wheels Phase

Access Level:
  • Approved apps/platforms only
  • Friend lists visible to parents
  • Regular content check-ins
Parent Actions:
  • Co-pilot mode: Sit together during online time
  • Practice scenarios: "What would you do if...?"
  • Normalize reporting problems
Teaching Focus: Platform mechanics, basic privacy, identifying suspicious interactions
14-16

Provisional License Phase

Access Level:
  • Broader platform access with monitoring
  • Private conversations respected but spot-checks happen
  • Screen time self-managed with accountability
Parent Actions:
  • Shift from checking phone to asking about experiences
  • Notice behavioral changes vs. reading every message
  • Discuss real scenarios from news/school
Teaching Focus: Critical evaluation, engagement manipulation, digital footprint awareness
16-18

Full License, Emergency Brake

Access Level:
  • Adult-level platform access
  • Privacy mostly respected unless concerning behavior
  • Expected self-regulation
Parent Actions:
  • Trust, but maintain verification ability if needed
  • Focus on life skills (research, evaluation)
  • Discuss complex topics (misinformation, echo chambers)
Teaching Focus: Professional presence, algorithmic awareness, digital citizenship

Remember:

Attackers rely on shame (so you don't tell), urgency (so you don't think), isolation (so you don't verify), and trust exploitation (so you drop your guard).

Your defenses: communication, verification, documentation, and instincts.
