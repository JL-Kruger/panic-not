INSTRUCTOR'S SPACE

Family Digital Literacy Workshop: 3SC Framework

Duration: 3 Hours (8 × 20-minute sessions with breaks) | Ages: 8-12 with families | Group Size: 10-15 participants

Core Framework: 3SC Principles

SAFE (Caution): Protective awareness and risk recognition
SANE (Courage): Balanced approach without fear paralysis
SAGE (Competence): Building practical skills and critical thinking

Pre-Workshop Setup (30 minutes before start)

Required Materials

  • Primary: Smartphones/tablets (minimum 1 per family)
  • Backup: Printed activity materials and physical demonstration props
  • Optional: PhyPhox app or browser access to sensor-js.xyz
  • Assessment: Family agreement templates and resource reference cards

Facilitator Preparation Checklist

  • Test all devices for sensor functionality
  • Prepare printed backup materials for technical failures
  • Set up 3SC framework visual displays
  • Review family composition for age-appropriate adaptations

SESSION 1: INTRODUCTION - DIGITAL CITIZENSHIP FOUNDATIONS (20 minutes)

Welcome & Framework Introduction (8 minutes)

Opening Context

“We’re here to become Digital Citizens who are SAFE, SANE, and SAGE in navigating our connected world. Today we’ll explore three interconnected digital realms that affect every family: smartphones, social internet spaces, and artificial intelligence systems.”

The 3SC Framework Explanation

SAFE (Caution): Recognizing risks without becoming fearful

  • Understanding what can go wrong
  • Building protective habits
  • Knowing when to ask for help

SANE (Courage): Balanced digital participation

  • Neither avoiding technology nor using it recklessly
  • Making informed decisions about digital activities
  • Maintaining healthy boundaries

SAGE (Competence): Developing practical wisdom

  • Building critical thinking about digital information
  • Understanding how technologies actually work
  • Teaching others and advocating for better policies

Family Team Formation (5 minutes)

  • Partner children (ages 8-12) with adults when possible
  • Establish “learning buddy” relationships for mixed groups
  • Set collaborative ground rules: questions welcome, learning together, no perfect answers required

Workshop Overview & Expectations (7 minutes)

Three-Hour Journey Map

  1. The Thing in Your Pocket (Smartphones): Understanding the sensor toolkit and privacy implications
  2. The Places It Can Take You (Social Internet): Navigating platform risks and information quality
  3. The Creatures You Meet There (AI Systems): Recognizing pattern-based intelligence and synthetic content

Success Indicators

By the end of today, families will:

  • Apply the 3SC framework to real digital decisions
  • Demonstrate practical safety skills across all three domains
  • Create family-specific digital agreements
  • Know when and how to seek additional support

Ground Rules & Participation Guidelines

  • Technology fails sometimes - we have backup plans
  • Different families have different comfort levels - that’s normal
  • Questions and concerns are valuable contributions
  • We learn more by doing than by listening

BREAK: 5 minutes

SESSION 2: SMARTPHONES PART 1 - THE SENSOR TOOLKIT (20 minutes)

Understanding Your Pocket Computer (12 minutes)

The Hidden Sensor Network (SAFE)

“Your smartphone contains 11+ different sensors that continuously collect information about you and your environment. Let’s discover what they are and what they can detect.”

Hands-On Discovery Activity:

  • Eyes (Cameras): Front and back cameras that can see more than human eyes
  • Ears (Microphones): Can hear sounds from multiple directions
  • Inner Ear (Accelerometer): Detects every movement and tilt
  • Internal Compass (Magnetometer): Knows magnetic north and can detect metal
  • Location Tracker (GPS): Pinpoints your exact position on Earth

Demonstration Options (choose based on available technology):

Option A - Advanced: Use PhyPhox accelerometer visualization

  • Have families perform “sensor dances” while watching motion data
  • Discuss: “What patterns is your phone learning about your daily activities?”

Option B - Standard: Built-in compass and level apps

  • Conduct “magnetic treasure hunt” around room
  • Demonstrate GPS location display (if appropriate for setting)

Option C - Backup: Physical props and role-play

  • Use actual compass, level, flashlight to show sensor functions
  • Family members act as different sensors reporting information

Privacy Implications Reality Check (SAGE)

Critical Understanding Points:

  • Sensors collect data automatically, even when you’re not actively using apps
  • Apps can access accelerometer data without security permissions, unlike cameras and microphones
  • Location tracking remains possible even when GPS is disabled through accelerometer and magnetometer data
  • Data collection enables behavioral profiling across multiple apps and platforms

Family Discussion Prompt: “If your phone can sense this much information, what should apps be allowed to do with it?”

Building Digital Awareness (SANE) (8 minutes)

Age-Appropriate Sensor Education

For Ages 8-9: Focus on concrete understanding

  • Sensors are like super-powered human senses
  • Some information should stay private (like your exact location)
  • Always ask trusted adults about sharing location with new apps

For Ages 10-11: Introduce privacy concepts

  • Understanding that sensor data creates “digital footprints”
  • Learning difference between helpful data use (navigation) vs concerning (tracking)
  • Practicing how to check and adjust privacy settings with adult help

For Ages 11-12: Develop critical evaluation skills

  • Analyzing which apps truly need which sensor permissions
  • Understanding how sensor data combines to create detailed profiles
  • Beginning to advocate for better privacy protections

Practical Skills Development

Family Activity: Privacy Settings Exploration

  • Open phone settings together (Settings > Privacy & Security on most devices)
  • Identify which apps have access to location, camera, microphone
  • Practice the “Why does this app need this?” question
  • Make one privacy adjustment together as practice

SESSION 3: SMARTPHONES PART 2 - SECURITY & FAMILY AGREEMENTS (20 minutes)

Expert-Recommended Security Configurations (SAGE) (10 minutes)

Platform-Specific Setup Guidance

iOS Family Security (if applicable):

  • Enable Screen Time with age-appropriate content restrictions
  • Configure Family Sharing for purchase approval
  • Set Communication Limits for school/bedtime hours
  • Review Find My location sharing for emergency purposes

Android Family Security (if applicable):

  • Set up Google Family Link with granular app controls
  • Configure Digital Wellbeing integration
  • Review Permission Manager for sensor access
  • Enable device location for family safety

Biometric Authentication Discussion (SANE)

Balanced Approach to Fingerprint/Face Recognition:

  • Benefits: Stronger security than simple passwords, convenient for children to use
  • Considerations: Biometric data receives different legal protections than passwords
  • Best Practice: Always maintain strong passcode backup authentication
  • Family Decision: Each family decides based on their comfort level and threat model

Creating Family Digital Agreements (SAFE) (10 minutes)

Essential Family Rules Framework

Device Location Rules: Where and when devices can be used

  • Common areas during specified times
  • Bedroom/bathroom restrictions based on family values
  • Charging station locations for overnight

Sharing Rules: What information can be shared and with whom

  • Photo sharing requires discussion with adults
  • Personal information (name, location, school) never shared online
  • New app downloads require adult approval and review

Problem Reporting Rules: How to handle concerning digital experiences

  • Immediate reporting without fear of punishment
  • Multiple trusted adults identified for different types of concerns
  • Documentation procedures (screenshots) when appropriate

Family Agreement Template Creation

Working Session: Each family begins drafting their specific rules

  • Adapt framework to individual family circumstances
  • Include consequences and rewards that make sense for each household
  • Plan for rule evolution as children demonstrate responsibility
  • Schedule regular family review sessions (recommended: monthly)

BREAK: 10 minutes

SESSION 4: SOCIAL INTERNET PART 1 - PLATFORM NAVIGATION SAFETY (20 minutes)

The Digital Neighborhood Framework (SAFE) (12 minutes)

Understanding Platform Geography

“Social media and gaming platforms are like different neighborhoods in a vast digital city. Some areas are safe for kids, others require adult supervision, and some are places children shouldn’t visit alone.”

Interactive Platform Mapping:

  • Draw simple “city map” showing different digital neighborhoods
  • Gaming Districts: Roblox, Minecraft, Fortnite - varying safety levels
  • Social Media Squares: TikTok, YouTube, Snapchat - age verification critical
  • Messaging Areas: Discord, WhatsApp - high risk for predator migration
  • Educational Zones: Khan Academy, educational apps - generally safer with oversight

Current Threat Assessment Reality

Gaming Platforms as Primary Risk (flagging with caution, not fear):

  • Roblox reported 13,316 child exploitation cases in 2023, with 24 arrests since 2018 for crimes involving children met through the platform
  • Voice chat and “social hangout” games present highest risk due to real-time communication bypassing text filters
  • 95% of predator communications migrate from gaming platforms to less moderated spaces like Discord

Platform Governance Reality Check:

  • Age verification systems are largely ineffective (self-reported birthdates)
  • Content moderation catches <60% of harmful content targeting children
  • Platform policies prioritize engagement over safety through design choices

The SPACES Safety System (SAGE)

Universal Platform Protection Framework:

S - Strangers: Online strangers remain strangers regardless of how friendly they seem P - Privacy Settings: Default settings favor data collection over child safety
A - Age Verification: Current systems provide minimal actual protection C - Content Moderation: All platforms have significant gaps in harmful content detection E - Evidence: Document concerning interactions immediately (screenshots, usernames, timestamps) S - Support Network: Maintain multiple trusted adults for different types of digital concerns

Practical Safety Implementation (SANE) (8 minutes)

Gaming Platform Specific Protections

Essential Safeguards (implement immediately):

  • Disable all voice chat features across gaming platforms
  • Restrict direct messaging to known real-life friends only
  • Require adult approval for all new contacts and friend requests
  • Understand that “private” game spaces facilitate predatory contact

Family Activity: Platform Safety Audit

  • Review current gaming apps/platforms children use
  • Check safety settings together on 1-2 platforms
  • Practice reporting mechanisms on platforms children access
  • Identify which platforms may need usage restrictions based on child’s age/maturity

Information Quality Foundations

Age-Appropriate Critical Thinking (concrete operational stage focus):

  • “Who Made This?” Detective Work: Identify creators and their motivations
  • Source Comparison Games: Same story from different sources to identify bias patterns
  • “Real Friend vs Screen Friend” Classification: Distinguishing online relationships from real-life connections

Algorithm Awareness Activity: “The Invisible DJ Concept”

  • Explain how platforms choose content like a radio DJ chooses songs
  • Demonstrate how platforms watch what you click and show more similar content
  • Practice “breaking the pattern” by deliberately seeking diverse content

SESSION 5: SOCIAL INTERNET PART 2 - COMMUNICATION & CRISIS RESPONSE (20 minutes)

Family Communication Excellence (SANE) (10 minutes)

The 5 Cs Communication Framework

Child-Centered Approaches: Recognize individual temperament and digital interests

  • Ages 8-9: Higher parent loyalty, need concrete examples
  • Ages 10-12: Focus on emotions/friendships, increased disclosure openness
  • Adapt communication style to each child’s developmental needs

Content Discussions: Focus on quality and age-appropriateness rather than prohibition Context Consideration: When, where, and how digital interactions occur matters Connection Focus: Emphasize healthy relationships and communication patterns Calm Regulation: Maintain emotional composure to encourage future disclosure

Conversation Starters for Specific Scenarios

For Cyberbullying: “I noticed you seemed upset after using your device. Sometimes people can be mean online. Has that happened to you?”

For Inappropriate Content: “Sometimes people put things online that aren’t meant for kids. Have you seen anything that made you uncomfortable?”

For Stranger Contact: “Who are the people you talk to online? Are they people you know in real life?”

Practice Session: Role-play one scenario with family teams, emphasizing non-judgmental response patterns

Crisis Response & Disclosure Protocols (SAFE) (10 minutes)

When Children Encounter Concerning Content

Evidence-Based Response Protocol:

  1. Stay calm - emotional reaction determines future disclosure willingness
  2. Thank them for telling you - reinforce reporting as the right choice
  3. Assess immediate safety - determine if urgent intervention needed
  4. Gather information through open-ended questions
  5. Validate emotional response - acknowledge their feelings as legitimate
  6. Follow up within 24 hours about feelings and any ongoing concerns

Professional Support Resource Navigation

Family-Level vs. Clinical Intervention Boundaries:

  • Family-appropriate: Teaching safety skills, having safety conversations, monitoring digital activity
  • Professional help needed: Persistent symptoms >1 month, interference with daily functioning, evidence of grooming/exploitation, expressions of self-harm

Essential Professional Credentials to Seek:

  • Licensed child psychologists with digital trauma experience
  • Family therapists with specialized child development training
  • Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) certification
  • Understanding of technology-related childhood trauma

Crisis Resource Directory

Immediate Crisis: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, local law enforcement Child Exploitation Reporting: National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (1-800-THE-LOST), Cybertipline.org Mental Health Support: SAMHSA Treatment Locator (findtreatment.gov, 1-800-662-HELP)

SESSION 6: AI SYSTEMS PART 1 - PATTERN RECOGNITION FUNDAMENTALS (20 minutes)

Understanding Artificial Intelligence as Pattern-Matching Systems (SAGE) (12 minutes)

Demystifying AI for Elementary Understanding

“AI systems are not magic or super-intelligent robots. They’re more like very advanced pattern-matching machines that can only recombine patterns they’ve seen before in new ways.”

The Four Types of AI Systems Children Encounter:

Chat AI (ChatGPT, Snapchat My AI, character chatbots):

  • Trained on billions of text conversations to predict likely human responses
  • Can provide information but hallucinate false facts 16-48% of the time
  • Cannot truly understand meaning or verify accuracy of information

Recommendation AI (YouTube, TikTok, social media algorithms):

  • Analyze user behavior patterns to predict engagement-driving content
  • Optimize for time spent rather than educational value or wellbeing
  • Can create “filter bubbles” that gradually introduce extreme content

Voice AI (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant):

  • Struggle significantly with children’s speech patterns (higher pitch, incomplete sentences)
  • Lack age-appropriate safety filtering, leading to potentially dangerous responses
  • Cannot distinguish between appropriate and inappropriate requests from children

Creation AI (image generators, “deepfake” tools, content creation):

  • Generate synthetic content by combining patterns from training data
  • Can create realistic fake images, videos, and audio of real people
  • Increasingly accessible through consumer apps requiring minimal technical skill

Current AI Threat Landscape Assessment (SAFE)

AI-Enhanced Child Targeting:

  • AI-generated child sexual abuse material increased dramatically in 2024, with over 20,000 synthetic images found on a single dark web forum in one month
  • Lancaster County case involved AI “nudify” apps creating explicit images of 20 high school students from social media photos
  • Voice cloning technology enables family impersonation for emergency scams

Social Media Algorithm Manipulation:

  • Platforms can achieve user dependency within 260 videos (under 35 minutes)
  • Algorithms can push harmful content to vulnerable users within 2.6-8 minutes
  • AI-powered content curation specifically targets developing psychology for engagement

AI Detection & Critical Evaluation (SANE) (8 minutes)

Age-Appropriate Detection Strategies

Note: Research shows human deepfake detection accuracy is only 55.54% overall, barely above chance - so focus on systematic questioning rather than claiming children can reliably detect AI content.

Elementary-Appropriate Detection Framework: Rather than teaching specific detection techniques (which become obsolete quickly), focus on:

The “STOP and Think” Method:

  • Source: Who created this and why?
  • Timing: When was this created?
  • Other sources: What do reliable sources say?
  • Purpose: What is this trying to make me think or do?

Practical Detection Activities (choose one based on group):

Option A - AI Detection Challenge:

  • Show 2-3 age-appropriate “Real or AI?” examples
  • Focus on questioning process rather than “correct” identification
  • Emphasize: “When in doubt, ask a trusted adult”

Option B - ChatBot Conversation Demo:

  • Demonstrate obviously AI responses with “textbook-like” answers
  • Show how AI cannot provide personal experiences or current information
  • Practice asking: “How do I know if I’m talking to a person or AI?”

Option C - Algorithm Awareness:

  • Explain why social media shows repetitive content (“If you click cat videos, you see more cat videos”)
  • Discuss benefits and limitations: “Is that good or bad? What else might you miss?”

Building AI Literacy Through Family Discussion

Key Understanding Points:

  • AI systems can be helpful tools when used with human supervision
  • AI cannot verify information accuracy - humans must fact-check AI responses
  • AI systems in social media prioritize engagement over child wellbeing
  • Children should always know when they’re interacting with AI vs humans

SESSION 7: AI SYSTEMS PART 2 - PRODUCTIVE COLLABORATION & FAMILY POLICIES (20 minutes)

Establishing AI Use Guidelines for Families (SAGE) (10 minutes)

When AI Enhances vs Replaces Learning

AI Enhancement (beneficial with supervision):

  • Brainstorming ideas for creative projects (with student development of concepts)
  • Research assistance for organizing information (with verification requirements)
  • Language support for grammar checking and vocabulary (with understanding focus)
  • Accessibility support for diverse learning needs (with teacher coordination)

AI Replacement (concerning patterns):

  • Completing homework without student input or understanding
  • Providing direct answers without showing reasoning processes
  • Creating final creative products without original student contribution
  • Solving problems without building student comprehension

Academic Integrity & Age-Appropriate Policies

Family AI Use Agreement Framework:

Disclosure Requirements: “I used AI to help with [specific task]” documentation Student Authorship: Final products must reflect student learning and decision-making Verification Responsibilities: AI information must be checked through reliable sources Learning Process Focus: Understanding matters more than perfect AI-generated outcomes

Implementation by Age Group:

  • Ages 8-9: All AI interaction requires adult supervision and pre-approval
  • Ages 10-11: Guided independent exploration with clear boundaries and disclosure
  • Ages 11-12: More complex AI-assisted projects with comprehensive attribution requirements

Convergent Threat Integration & Family Planning (SAFE) (10 minutes)

Understanding Intersection Attacks

How Smartphone, Social Media, and AI Threats Combine:

  • AI systems enhance smartphone sensor data analysis for behavioral profiling
  • Social media photos become source material for AI-generated synthetic content
  • Cross-platform data sharing creates comprehensive targeting capabilities
  • Voice cloning from social media posts enables family impersonation attacks

Family Protection Integration Strategy:

  • Device Security: Sensor permission management + biometric authentication
  • Platform Safety: SPACES framework + information verification habits
  • AI Awareness: Detection questioning + productive use guidelines
  • Communication: Regular check-ins + crisis response protocols

Future-Proofing Family Digital Safety

Adaptive Framework Development:

  • Quarterly Safety Reviews: Reassess rules and threats as technology evolves
  • Growth Mindset: Focus on learning together rather than achieving perfect understanding
  • Community Connections: Link with schools and other families for ongoing support
  • Advocacy Preparation: Understand when individual family measures are insufficient

Warning Signs for Additional Support:

  • Technology-related anxiety or behavioral changes lasting >2 weeks
  • Evidence of predatory contact or financial exploitation attempts
  • Child requesting help with concerning online experiences
  • Family conflict about digital boundaries exceeding normal adjustment difficulties

Creating Sustainable Family Practices

Weekly Family Tech Check-ins: 15-minute conversations using provided frameworks

  • What worked well with digital activities this week?
  • Any concerns or questions about online experiences?
  • Any apps/platforms want to try or settings want to adjust?
  • Family rule updates needed based on maturity/responsibility demonstration?

Program Adaptation Guidelines

For Lower Technical Literacy Groups: Focus on concrete activities, simplified language, more visual demonstrations, increased peer support pairing For Higher Risk Communities: Emphasize immediate safety implementation, provide more crisis resource details, adapt to specific local threat patterns For Mixed Age Groups: Offer differentiated activities within framework, use older participants as mentors, adapt complexity based on developmental readiness

This lesson plan integrates validated research with practical family implementation while maintaining appropriate caution about accuracy and avoiding fear-based approaches to digital safety education.
